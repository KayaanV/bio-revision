CIE 9700 · Topic 1 — Cell Structure

Which organelles are double-membrane bound?

Only three. Learn these as a set — everything else is single-membrane or no membrane.

A double membrane means the organelle is enclosed by two phospholipid bilayers (an outer and an inner membrane, with a gap between them). The trick for the exam is that this small group all relates to either the genetic material or to energy.

MembranesOrganellesKey detail to quote
Double ✓✓ Nucleus, Mitochondrion, Chloroplast Nucleus → the two membranes form the nuclear envelope (with nuclear pores). Mitochondrion → inner membrane folded into cristae. Chloroplast → envelope plus internal thylakoid membranes.
Single ✓ Rough & smooth ER, Golgi body, lysosome, vesicle, plant vacuole (tonoplast) One bilayer only. (The cell-surface membrane is also a single bilayer.)
None ✗ Ribosome (80S/70S), centriole, nucleolus, cell wall No membrane at all — a common trap in MCQs.
Memory hook"N-M-C": Nucleus, Mitochondrion, Chloroplast. All three are thought to be linked to endosymbiosis (mitochondria & chloroplasts) or wrap the DNA (nucleus) — which is exactly why they ended up with two membranes.

Typical sizes: prokaryotes vs eukaryotes

Units first: 1 mm = 1000 µm, and 1 µm = 1000 nm. Get these conversions wrong and every size question collapses.

StructureApproximate sizeIn one unit
Small molecule (e.g. glucose, amino acid)~1 nm0.001 µm
Cell-surface membrane (thickness)~7 nm0.007 µm
Ribosome~25 nm0.025 µm
Virus20 – 300 nm0.02 – 0.3 µm
Bacterium (prokaryote)0.5 – 5 µm—
Mitochondrion / chloroplast1 – 10 µm—
Eukaryotic cell (animal / plant)10 – 100 µm—

The headline comparison the examiner wants: a eukaryotic cell is roughly 10× larger (in length) than a typical prokaryotic cell, and a virus is roughly 10× smaller again than a prokaryote. So the order of magnitude runs: virus (nm) → bacterium (~1 µm) → eukaryotic cell (~10–100 µm).

Worked conversion
A bacterium of 2 µm and a virus of 100 nm.
100 nm = 100 ÷ 1000 = 0.1 µm.
So the bacterium (2 µm) is 20× the length of the virus.
CIE 9700 · Topic 1 — Cell Structure

The structure of a virus

Viruses are non-cellular: no cytoplasm, no organelles, no ribosomes, no nucleus — and only metabolically active inside a host. The syllabus wants the three core parts.

The parts to learn

  • Nucleic acid core — the genetic material: either DNA or RNA (never both), which carries the genes needed to make new viruses.
  • Capsid — a protein coat built from many identical protein subunits called capsomeres. It protects the nucleic acid and gives the virus its shape.
  • Lipid envelope (some viruses only) — an outer membrane stolen from the host cell's membrane as the virus leaves it. Studded with glycoprotein "spikes" that bind to specific receptors on a host cell.
Don't writeViruses have no cytoplasm, no ribosomes, no organelles and no cell-surface membrane of their own (the envelope is borrowed). They cannot replicate independently — they must hijack a host cell's machinery.
CIE 9700 · Topic 1 — Cell Structure (microscopy & calibration)

Eyepiece graticule & stage micrometer

This is genuinely the most-misunderstood practical idea at AS. Read the one-line summary, then the mechanism.

The whole idea in one sentenceThe eyepiece graticule is a ruler with no units, so you use the stage micrometer (a ruler with known units) once to find out how many micrometres each eyepiece division is worth — then you put the micrometer away and measure your specimen.

What each tool is

ToolWhere it sitsScaleProblem / role
Eyepiece graticuleInside the eyepiece (so it overlays whatever you look at)Usually 100 evenly-spaced divisions, arbitrary — no real unitsYou measure the specimen in these divisions, but 1 division means nothing until calibrated.
Stage micrometerA slide placed on the stage (where a specimen would go)An accurately engraved scale, typically 1 mm split into 100, so 1 division = 0.01 mm = 10 µmProvides a known real length to calibrate the eyepiece graticule against.

Why calibration is needed — and why at each magnification

The eyepiece graticule is in the eyepiece, so its divisions always look the same size no matter which objective lens you use. But the specimen gets magnified more by a higher-power objective. So the real length represented by one eyepiece division changes every time you change the objective lens. That is why you must re-calibrate at every magnification.

How you use them together (the method)

  1. Place the stage micrometer on the stage and focus on it.
  2. Line up the two scales so the eyepiece graticule sits over the stage micrometer.
  3. Count how many eyepiece divisions match a known number of stage-micrometer divisions.
  4. Convert: each stage division = 10 µm, so you can work out the real length of one eyepiece division at this magnification.
  5. Remove the stage micrometer, put your real specimen on the stage, and measure it in eyepiece divisions.
  6. Multiply: real size = (eyepiece divisions measured) × (µm per eyepiece division).
Full worked example
Stage micrometer: 1 mm ÷ 100 → 1 stage division = 10 µm.
You observe: 20 eyepiece divisions line up exactly with 5 stage-micrometer divisions.
5 stage divisions = 5 × 10 = 50 µm.
So 20 eyepiece divisions = 50 µm → 1 eyepiece division = 50 ÷ 20 = 2.5 µm.
Now your cell measures 30 eyepiece divisions across.
Real width = 30 × 2.5 = 75 µm.
CIE 9700 · Topic 6 — Nucleic Acids (with Topic 2 biological molecules)

Ribose vs deoxyribose

Both are pentose (5-carbon) sugars in ring form. The entire difference is at carbon 2.

  • Ribose → –OH on carbon 2′ → found in RNA (and in ATP, NAD, etc.).
  • Deoxyribose → –H on carbon 2′ (one fewer oxygen) → found in DNA.

That missing oxygen makes DNA slightly more chemically stable — useful for a molecule that has to store information long-term.

CIE 9700 · Topic 6 — Nucleic Acids & DNA Replication

DNA replication & directionality

The key fact everything hangs on: the two strands are antiparallel, and DNA polymerase only builds in one direction (5′ → 3′).

Quick correctionYou wrote "ligand" — the enzyme you mean is DNA ligase. (A ligand is something else entirely: a molecule that binds to a receptor or a central metal ion. Different word, different topic.)

Step by step

  1. Helicase unwinds the double helix and breaks the hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs, exposing two template strands.
  2. DNA polymerase adds free DNA nucleotides to the exposed templates by complementary base pairing (A–T, C–G), catalysing phosphodiester bonds in the sugar–phosphate backbone.
  3. Crucially, DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to the 3′ end — it builds the new strand 5′ → 3′, reading the template 3′ → 5′.

Leading vs lagging — and why

Because the strands are antiparallel, only one template runs the "right" way for continuous synthesis:

  • Leading strand — its template runs 3′→5′ toward the fork, so DNA polymerase follows the fork and builds it continuously in one piece.
  • Lagging strand — its template runs the "wrong" way, so DNA polymerase works away from the fork in short pieces called Okazaki fragments, restarting as the fork opens.
  • DNA ligase then joins (catalyses the phosphodiester bonds between) the Okazaki fragments into one continuous strand.

Each new molecule ends up with one original strand and one new strand — this is semi-conservative replication (proved next, in Meselson–Stahl).

CIE 9700 · Topic 6 — Nucleic Acids (evidence for semi-conservative replication)

The Meselson–Stahl experiment

It proved DNA replicates semi-conservatively by tracking heavy vs light nitrogen through the generations.

The nitrogen trick

Nitrogen is part of every DNA base. There are two forms (isotopes):

  • ¹⁵N — "heavy" nitrogen → makes denser (heavier) DNA.
  • ¹⁴N — "light" nitrogen (normal) → makes less dense (lighter) DNA.

By spinning the DNA in a density-gradient centrifuge, denser DNA settles lower in the tube and lighter DNA settles higher. So the position of the band tells you the density — and therefore which nitrogen the DNA contains.

What they did

  1. Grew bacteria for many generations in a medium containing only ¹⁵N → all DNA was heavy.
  2. Switched them to a medium containing only ¹⁴N, so all new strands made from now on are light.
  3. Extracted and centrifuged the DNA after each round of replication.

The results — and what they rule out

GenerationBand(s) seenWhat it tells you
Gen 0 (start)One heavy bandAll DNA is ¹⁵N/¹⁵N — the baseline.
Gen 1 (1 replication)One intermediate bandEvery molecule = one ¹⁵N (old) strand + one ¹⁴N (new) strand. This rules out conservative replication (which would give a heavy band and a light band, not one in-between).
Gen 2 (2 replications)Half intermediate, half light (two bands)The light band appearing rules out dispersive replication (which would give a single, ever-lighter intermediate band, never a fully light one).
ConclusionOnly semi-conservative replication fits both results: each new DNA molecule keeps one original parent strand and gains one newly-built strand.
CIE 9700 · Topic 4 — Cell Membranes & Transport

How cholesterol regulates membrane fluidity

Cholesterol is a fluidity buffer: it stops the membrane from getting too fluid when hot, and too rigid when cold.

Cholesterol is a small, fairly rigid lipid (a steroid) that sits between the phospholipid molecules, with its hydrophobic part tucked among the fatty-acid tails. Because it interacts with those tails, it controls how freely the phospholipids can move.

TemperatureProblem without cholesterolWhat cholesterol does
High temperaturePhospholipids move too much → membrane becomes too fluid & leakyHolds the tails together, restricts their movement → reduces fluidity, keeps the membrane stable and less permeable.
Low temperaturePhospholipids pack tightly → membrane becomes too rigid / solidifiesSits between them and stops them packing too closely → maintains fluidity, prevents the membrane setting solid.

So across a range of temperatures cholesterol keeps fluidity roughly constant. As a bonus it increases mechanical stability and reduces the membrane's permeability to small water-soluble molecules and ions (e.g. Na⁺, H⁺), because it fills gaps between phospholipids.

Composition & structure — and how each links to function

  • Xylem vessel element — dead, hollow, empty cells joined end-to-end with the end walls broken down, forming a continuous tube. Walls are thickened and waterproofed with lignin (laid down in rings/spirals or as pits). No living contents = no resistance to flow. Function: transports water and dissolved mineral ions up the plant, and gives support.
  • Phloem sieve-tube element — living but stripped down: no nucleus, only a thin layer of cytoplasm, few organelles, no ribosomes. Joined end-to-end by sieve plates (perforated end walls with sieve pores) so sap can flow through. Function: transports assimilates (mainly sucrose and amino acids) — translocation. The empty centre lowers resistance to flow.
  • Companion cell — a fully living cell with a nucleus, dense cytoplasm, many mitochondria and many ribosomes, linked to its sieve-tube element by plasmodesmata. Function: carries out the metabolic work the sieve tube can't (it has no nucleus), and uses ATP from its many mitochondria for the active loading of sucrose into the phloem.
Why the mitochondria matterActive loading of sucrose needs ATP → that is exactly why companion cells have many mitochondria. A classic exam link.

CIE 9700 · Topic 8 — Transport in Mammals (the cardiac cycle)

The Wiggers diagram, fully explained

This single diagram shows pressure in the left atrium, left ventricle and aorta, plus ventricular volume, across one heartbeat. Once you can read it, every cardiac-cycle question becomes a "which line is highest?" question.

The golden rule that explains every valve

Valves obey pressure, nothing elseA valve opens when the pressure behind it is higher than in front, and snaps shut the instant pressure in front becomes higher (i.e. when blood tries to flow backwards). Every event below is just this rule applied.

Walking through one beat

  1. Atrial systole — atria contract, atrial pressure rises slightly above ventricular, topping up the ventricles. This sets the end-diastolic volume (EDV ≈ 120 cm³) — the most blood the ventricle holds.
  2. AV valves close → first heart sound "lub" (S1). Ventricles start contracting, so ventricular pressure rises above atrial → the atrioventricular valves shut to stop backflow into the atria.
  3. Isovolumetric contraction — for a brief moment all valves are closed, so pressure shoots up but volume stays constant (notice the flat top of the volume line).
  4. Semilunar valves open — once ventricular pressure exceeds aortic pressure (~80 mmHg), the aortic/pulmonary valves open and blood is ejected. Ventricular volume falls sharply.
  5. Ejection — pressure peaks (~120 mmHg) and aortic pressure rises with it. Volume drops to the end-systolic volume (ESV ≈ 50 cm³).
  6. Semilunar valves close → second heart sound "dub" (S2). The ventricle relaxes, its pressure drops below aortic, so blood tries to flow back → the semilunar valves shut. The brief backflow against the closed valve causes the small bump on the aortic curve: the dicrotic notch.
  7. Isovolumetric relaxation — again all valves closed, pressure plummets while volume stays constant.
  8. AV valves open → filling. When ventricular pressure falls below atrial pressure, the AV valves open and the ventricles fill passively, ready for the next beat.

Everything you can be asked to deduce from it

You're asked…How to read it off the diagram
When do the AV valves close/open?Close where ventricular pressure crosses above atrial (start of contraction, S1); open where ventricular falls below atrial (start of filling).
When do the semilunar valves open/close?Open where ventricular crosses above aortic; close where ventricular falls below aortic (S2, dicrotic notch).
What causes the heart sounds?S1 ("lub") = AV valves closing; S2 ("dub") = semilunar valves closing.
Stroke volume?EDV − ESV (here ≈ 120 − 50 = 70 cm³).
Duration of a cardiac cycle / heart rate?Read the time for one full cycle off the x-axis; heart rate = 60 ÷ (cycle time in s). A 0.8 s cycle → 75 beats min⁻¹.
When is the ventricle contracting vs relaxing?Rising ventricular pressure = systole (contracting); falling = diastole (relaxing).
Why does aortic pressure never fall to zero?Elastic recoil of the artery walls maintains pressure between beats — that's the slow decline of the blue line.

Tissue fluid: formation & composition

Think of tissue fluid as blood plasma that has leaked out — minus the cells and minus the big proteins.

Composition — and how it differs from blood

ComponentBlood plasmaTissue fluid(Lymph)
Red blood cells✓✗✗
Platelets✓✗✗
White blood cells✓ manyfew✓ many (lymphocytes)
Large plasma proteins
(albumin, fibrinogen)		✓ lots✗ / very littlelittle
Water✓✓✓
Glucose, amino acids, ions, O₂✓✓✓
CO₂ & other wastessome✓ (returning)some
 The two big differences from blood: tissue fluid has no red blood cells or platelets (too big to leave the capillary) and almost no plasma proteins (also too big). Everything small enough to filter through — water, glucose, ions, oxygen — is present.

How tissue fluid is formed

  1. At the arterial end of the capillary, the heart generates a high hydrostatic pressure. This is greater than the opposing effects, so it forces fluid out through the small gaps in the capillary wall — this is ultrafiltration. The fluid that leaves (plasma minus cells minus large proteins) becomes tissue fluid.
  2. Large plasma proteins stay behind in the capillary because they're too big to pass through. This makes the blood's water potential more negative (lower) than that of the tissue fluid.
  3. At the venous end, hydrostatic pressure has dropped (fluid has been lost and there's friction along the way). Now the blood's lower water potential dominates, so water moves back into the capillary by osmosis, down the water-potential gradient.
  4. Most tissue fluid returns this way. The excess (~10%) drains into the lymphatic system and is eventually returned to the blood.
Don't confuse the two forcesHydrostatic pressure = the physical "push" from blood pressure (drives fluid out). Water potential / oncotic effect = the osmotic "pull" caused by proteins staying in the blood (draws water back in).

Trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli

As you go deeper, the airways lose cartilage and goblet cells, and the wall gets thinner — all building toward efficient gas exchange at the alveoli.

FeatureTracheaBronchiBronchiolesAlveoli
Cartilage✓ C-shaped rings✓ irregular blocks✗✗

Goblet cells✓✓few → none in smallest✗

Epitheliumciliated columnarciliated columnarciliated → flattenedsquamous (thin, single)

What each tissue is for

  • Cartilage — strong but flexible; holds the airways open and stops them collapsing when pressure drops during breathing in. C-shaped in the trachea so the oesophagus behind can expand.
  • Smooth muscle — contracts to narrow the airway (controls air flow / diameter). It can't widen the airway itself…
  • Elastic fibres — …which is where these come in. When the muscle relaxes, the stretched elastic fibres recoil and widen the airway again. In the alveoli, recoil also helps push air out when breathing out. (Muscle and elastic fibres work antagonistically.)
  • Goblet cells — secrete mucus that traps dust, pathogens and debris.
  • Cilia — beat to sweep the trapped mucus up towards the throat to be swallowed or removed (the "mucociliary escalator").
  • Alveolar (squamous) epithelium — a single layer of flattened cells giving a very thin wall → short diffusion distance for efficient gas exchange, surrounded by capillaries.
CIE 9700 · Topic 11 — Immunity

Antibody structure

Antibodies (immunoglobulins) are Y-shaped glycoproteins made by plasma cells. Learn the four chains, the two regions, and the binding sites.

The parts to learn

  • Four polypeptide chains — two long heavy chains and two short light chains, joined by disulfide bonds (disulfide bridges).
  • Variable region — at the tips of the Y. Its amino-acid sequence (and therefore its 3-D shape) is different in every antibody, forming two identical antigen-binding sites that are complementary to one specific antigen (lock-and-key).
  • Constant region — the same for all antibodies of a given class. It allows the antibody to bind to receptors on phagocytes and other immune cells.
  • Hinge region — gives the molecule flexibility, so the two arms can move apart to bind two antigens (e.g. on two separate pathogens, helping clump them together — agglutination).
Two binding sites, one jobBecause there are two antigen-binding sites, one antibody can latch onto two pathogens at once and agglutinate (clump) them, making them easier for phagocytes to engulf.
